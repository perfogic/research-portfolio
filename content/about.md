## Brief Description

I graduated two years ago with an Engineering degree in Computer Science from Hanoi University of Science and Technology, Vietnam, with Excellent classification.

My path into blockchain began around four years ago, while I was still a student. Since then, I have worked on several types of blockchain systems, including NFT marketplaces, DeFi systems such as AMM V3, V4 design, and cross-chain bridge systems for Cosmos-to-TON and Bitcoin-to-Cosmos interoperability, involving light-client and state-verification mechanisms.

This bridge work was especially important to me because it required me to understand how light clients are built around a chain’s consensus assumptions, how commit signatures provide evidence that a block was agreed upon by sufficient validator voting power, and how state proofs can show that certain data belongs to the resulting on-chain state.

Studying Cosmos-based chains and working on chain-level infrastructure made me realize that I wanted to pursue work closer to blockchain core protocols, with the hope of contributing more directly to the foundations of decentralized systems.

## Research Journey and Reading Narrative

#### 1. My First Research Journey

In 2025, I had the opportunity to work with Professor David Tran. Through that collaboration, I studied distributed networks more deeply through the lens of Data Availability Sampling. One outcome of this work was my first paper, _Efficient Data Availability Sampling via Coded Distributed Arrays_, co-authored with Hung Vuong Huu and Duc A. Tran, which was accepted to IEEE ICBC 2026.

As this was my first research paper, many aspects of the process were new to me. I had to spend a significant amount of time reading background material, catching up with the relevant concepts, and slowly building the context needed to contribute to the work. I was fortunate that the project eventually led to the outcome we hoped for. I have documented part of this research process in my DAS research log [here](research.html#das_research_log).

#### 2. Research Narrative

As I became more interested in blockchain core protocols, I started looking for material that could help me build a stronger foundation in consensus and distributed systems. One of the first books I chose for this purpose was Professor Vincent Gramoli’s _Blockchain Scalability and Its Foundations in Distributed Systems_.

The book gave me a clearer initial map of consensus algorithms, but it also made me realize how much more I wanted to explore beyond the book itself through research papers. I then started reading additional papers on leader-based BFT protocols, using classical designs as a baseline and gradually moving toward more recent papers on latency, leader replacement, responsiveness, and communication complexity.

On top of that, I also explored leaderless consensus through further papers, which helped me develop a deeper understanding of leaderless protocols. Moreover, Ethereum’s single-slot finality roadmap gave me a concrete reason to study fast-path and one-voting-phase BFT protocols.

I have documented this reading process in my consensus reading log [here](research.html#consensus_reading_log). My hope is that these notes can be useful not only as a record of my own learning path, but also as a possible entry point for other newcomers who come across my profile and want to explore these topics.

## Research Attitude

A sentence I often remind myself of is: “You’re always in somebody’s school.”

I take this as a reminder to stay humble while learning. I enjoy studying new ideas, but I try to prioritize real understanding over impressive terminology. My goal is to read carefully, ask precise questions, and keep learning from people who understand the field more deeply.
